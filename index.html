Introduction to Lasers

Laser stands for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. It is a device that produces a strong, monochromatic, collimated, and highly coherent beam of optical radiation by stimulating electronic, ionic, or molecular transitions to higher energy levels. The first laser was built by Theodore H. Maiman in 1960 using a ruby crystal as the active medium.

Properties of Lasers

  • Monochromatic: Emission of a single wavelength of light.
  • Coherent: All photons have a constant phase relationship.
  • Highly Directional: Produces a very tight, unidirectional beam.
  • High Intensity: Suitable for applications such as cutting and welding.
  • Tight Focusing: Can be focused to a few micrometers, useful in surgery and material processing.
  • Spectral Purity: Extremely small spectral width, making it useful in holography and optical communication.

Basic Principles

Laser operation is based on three main principles:

Absorption

Atoms absorb energy, causing electrons to move to a higher energy state.

Spontaneous Emission

Excited atoms return to a lower state randomly, emitting photons incoherently.

Stimulated Emission

When an incident photon of correct frequency interacts with an excited atom, it induces emission of a second photon with identical properties, leading to amplification of light.

Derivation of Relation Between Einstein's Coefficients

Einstein introduced three coefficients to describe the interaction of atoms with radiation:

  • \( B_{12} \): Probability coefficient for absorption
  • \( A_{21} \): Probability coefficient for spontaneous emission
  • \( B_{21} \): Probability coefficient for stimulated emission

Step 1: Transition Probabilities

The rate of absorption per unit volume is given by:

\[ W_{12} = B_{12} u(\nu) N_1 \]

The rate of spontaneous emission per unit volume is:

\[ W_{21}^{sp} = A_{21} N_2 \]

The rate of stimulated emission per unit volume is:

\[ W_{21}^{st} = B_{21} u(\nu) N_2 \]

At thermal equilibrium, the number of upward transitions must equal the number of downward transitions:

\[ B_{12} u(\nu) N_1 = A_{21} N_2 + B_{21} u(\nu) N_2 \]

Step 2: Using the Boltzmann Distribution

The population of energy states is given by the Boltzmann distribution:

\[ \frac{N_2}{N_1} = e^{-h\nu / kT} \]

Step 3: Using Planck's Radiation Law

The energy density \( u(\nu) \) is given by Planck’s radiation law:

\[ u(\nu) = \frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu / kT} - 1} \]

Step 4: Deriving the Relationship

Comparing terms, we obtain:

\[ \frac{A_{21}}{B_{21}} = \frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3} \]

This equation shows that the probability of spontaneous emission increases as the cube of the frequency.

Population Inversion in Lasers

Population inversion is a condition where the number of atoms in a higher energy state (N₂) exceeds the number in a lower energy state (N₁). This condition is essential for laser action.

Figure 3: Illustration of Population Inversion in Laser Operation.

Under normal thermal equilibrium, most atoms reside in the lower energy state (N₁ > N₂), leading to absorption rather than stimulated emission. To achieve laser action, external energy is supplied via pumping, forcing more atoms into the excited state (N₂ > N₁).

If N₁ > N₂, spontaneous emission dominates, and no laser action occurs. However, when N₂ > N₁, stimulated emission prevails, amplifying light and leading to laser generation.

Conditions for Population Inversion

  • There must be an external energy source to excite atoms (pumping).
  • Atoms should stay in the excited state long enough to allow stimulated emission.
  • The system should avoid spontaneous emission domination.

Pumping in Lasers

The process of moving the atoms from their ground state to an excited state is called pumping. The objective is to obtain a non-thermal equilibrium and achieve population inversion.

Types of Pumping

Optical Pumping

In optical pumping, atoms are excited by bombarding them with photons. An example of a laser using optical pumping is the Ruby Laser.

Electrical Pumping

In electrical pumping, atoms are excited by electron collisions in a discharge tube. An example of a laser using electrical pumping is the Helium-Neon (He-Ne) Laser.

Continuous Wave and Pulsed Lasers

  • Lasers that maintain a population inversion indefinitely produce continuous output and are called Continuous Wave (CW) Lasers.
  • Lasers that have a short-lived population inversion produce pulsed output and are called Pulsed Lasers.

He-Ne and Ruby Lasers

He-Ne Laser (Four Level Laser)

The Helium-Neon (He-Ne) laser was the first continuous laser, invented by Ali Javan in 1961. It consists of a mixture of helium and neon gases and operates in continuous wave (CW) mode.

Construction of He-Ne Laser

  • Active Medium: A gas mixture of helium and neon in a 5:1 to 20:1 ratio contained in a glass tube.
  • Pumping Mechanism: Excitation occurs via electrical discharge of around 1000 volts.
  • Resonant Cavity: Consists of a discharge tube with high-reflecting mirrors at both ends.

Figure 2: Construction and Energy Levels of a Helium-Neon Laser.

Working of He-Ne Laser

When power is switched on, electrons excite helium atoms, which transfer energy to neon atoms via collisions. The excited neon atoms undergo stimulated emission, producing a coherent laser beam at 632.8 nm (red light). The beam is amplified through multiple reflections inside the cavity and exits through a partially silvered mirror.

Ruby Laser (Three Level Laser)

The Ruby laser, invented by Theodore Maiman in 1960, is a solid-state laser that uses a synthetic ruby crystal as its gain medium.

Construction of Ruby Laser

  • Active Medium: A cylindrical ruby rod (Al₂O₃ doped with Cr³⁺ ions).
  • Pumping Source: A xenon flash lamp excites chromium ions to higher energy states.
  • Optical Cavity: The rod is placed between two mirrors (one fully reflective, one partially reflective).

Figure 1: Energy Level Diagram of a Ruby Laser.

Working of Ruby Laser

Optical pumping excites Cr³⁺ ions to a higher energy state. These ions transition to a metastable state before undergoing stimulated emission, producing a pulsed laser beam at 694.3 nm (red light). The emitted photons reflect within the optical cavity, amplifying the laser output.

Applications of Lasers

  • Medical: Eye surgery, dental treatment, skin therapy.
  • Industrial: Cutting, welding, material processing.
  • Scientific: Holography, spectroscopy, optical communication.
  • Military: Targeting, missile guidance, defense systems.
  • Consumer Electronics: CD/DVD players, laser printers, bar code scanners.
